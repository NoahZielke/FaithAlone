A List of Unbiblical Roman Catholic Accretions

The fundamental reason for the vast gulf between Biblical Christianity and Roman Catholicism is our respective authorities:

  • Biblical Christianity teaches that inspired Scripture is the sole infallible rule of faith (2 Timothy 3:16-17). Not the only rule of faith - as is sometimes erroneously alleged - the only infallible rule of faith
  • Roman Catholicism teaches that the Roman Catholic Church's judgment of Scripture and Tradition is the sole infallible rule of faith

This Roman Catholic (and to a lesser, more disorganized degree, Eastern Orthodox) doctrine of Sola Ecclesia has led to many developments (accretions) in their religion, which no one reading the Bible, and practicing any meaningful form of exegesis, would ever get from the text.

With Biblical doctrine, one must show a chapter and verse in inspired Scripture that clearly states what they are trying to prove, in context. Allusion isn't good enough. Symbology is not good enough. We build our theology on clear statements, from what we know is God-breathed. That is how we avoid accretions, and heresy.

In contrast, the case for these Roman Catholic doctrines is established on taking a verse of Scripture, which does not at all, in context, state their doctrine, or anything like their doctrine, and then immediately running to the traditions of non-inspired people from centuries after the Apostles, and selectively citing what they said, in order to arrive at the fully fleshed-out Roman Catholic doctrine. Sometimes, a verse of Scripture is not even appealed to, but rather Tradition alone.

To justify this practice, Catholics insist that the corpus of traditions which they maintain are directly from the Apostles themselves, and therefore, represent authoritative elements of the faith outside of Scripture. The two major flaws with these assertions are:

  • Not everything an Apostle did or taught was correct, or inspired (Galatians 2:11-14, Acts 16:1-3)
  • No direct Apostolic link can be established for any of these dogmas

One of the first instances in the history of the Christian Church of someone claiming to have a direct Apostolic basis for a doctrine is Irenaeus (130-202 AD), in his work Against Heresies, stating that Jesus was over 40 years old when He died. No one on earth believes that today. Everyone agrees that he was teaching false doctrine. And, he is one of the earliest Christian writers. "An Apostle told me", isn't good enough.

Further, Roman Catholics freely disregard claimed Apostolic tradition if it doesn't agree with what they already believe. They agree with Pope Victor I (189-199 AD) in his attempt to force the entire Church to celebrate Passover on a single day (in direct violation of Romans 14:4-6), even though a bishop named Polycrates (130-196 AD) cited an Apostolic tradition from the Apostles Philip and John for the day on which he celebrated Passover, when rejecting Victor's judgment (Eusebius - Ecclesiastical History, Book 5, Chapter 24). Do Roman Catholics care? No, they do not, instead siding with Victor against the Apostolic tradition, and the Bible.

And, not even the most basic questions about any of these supposedly Apostolic traditions which Roman Catholics wish to bind to people's consciences can be answered, such as - which Apostle? Who did he tell it to? When did he say it? Where was he? Is the person who he supposedly told it to reliable? Is each person in the chain of transmitters after him reliable? Is the manuscript tradition of the relevant sources robust enough to bind them on the conscience of all Christians? These basic questions, whose answers should be forthcoming if the tradition was legitimate, and binding on the souls of all Christians, cannot be answered for even a single one of any of these traditions.

Therefore, when reading Church history, the writers are to be subject to the same scrutiny that writers today are subject to, namely, "Where in Scripture did they get that from?". Age isn't good enough. There were people teaching lies, and creating false doctrine, while the New Testament itself was being written (John 21:21-23, Acts 15:23-24, 20:28-31: 2 Corinthians 2:17, Galatians 1:6-7: 2 Peter 3:16, Revelation 2:15). "We all believe it", isn't good enough. Those who are concerned with the truth should require inspired proof for anything that anyone tries to bind to their (and their family's) conscience.

Appendix I - The Nebulous and Uncertain Nature of Tradition

Briefly, it should be noted that there is uncertainty and controversy over the text of Ecumenical Councils, with missing or incomplete acts, textual variants, or variants between Greek and Latin copies, for instance. Additionally, it should not have to be mentioned that historically these texts were not available for the average Christian, in their own language, yet, Roman Catholics hold that at least portions of them are inspired by the Holy Spirit.

When these considerations are extended to local councils, and the writings of individual early Christians, the problem explodes in complexity and controversy. Entire works are forged, and written in the names of popular preachers, leading later writers to mistakenly cite them as authentic, and some works survive only in translation, or otherwise have a dubious manuscript history. Infamous forgeries like the Donation of Constantine, and the Pseudo-Isidorian Decretals have impacted world history, because of their acceptance and use by the Papacy.

This consideration is highlighted to emphasize that not only is "tradition" a very nebulous category, subject to the whims of whoever is claiming to define it, but the texts that constitute "tradition" are often contested, and very often, unread by more than 99% of Christians at any given time, especially historically, being locked away from most of them by impediments such as availability, and language barriers.
