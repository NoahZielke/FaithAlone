Thoughts on Determinism

  • If every cause has only one potential effect, then determinism is true
    • In this case, if the universe were simulated 10,000 times, everything would happen in the exact same way every time, because there is no uncertainty in the system, even if the system is very complex
    • This would mean that having consciousness is more like being a "spectator" rather than an "agent", because there would be no real agency. Instead, every thought/action would just be the unfolding of something which was mathematically certain from the very beginning of the universe
      • This has troubling implications if one believes in God, because it would mean that nobility/wickedness are totally out of the control of whatever "agents" happen to embody those characteristics. It would also mean that whoever created the initial conditions would be entirely responsible for every action that resulted afterwards, and that every action was therefore in the direct will of that creator, down to the meticulous details of events, including outrageous suffering. Additionally, every "agent" (spectator having a subjective experience of consciousness) in that creation would be entirely designed and controlled by that creator, for their entire lifetime
  • If any mechanism in the universe introduces probability, then determinism is false
    • Suppose that radioactive decay and the positions of electrons operate according to probability - where different future outcomes are genuinely possible at a given time. This introduces "randomness"/chaos (i.e. non-determinism) into the system, even if it can be modeled according to a probability, and even if its mechanism/cause is fully identified and understood. "Randomness" does not necessarily mean "mystery".

      One could imagine, for example, a mechanism which produces particle A 80% of the time, and particle B 20% of the time. Over a sufficiently long period of time, one expects the produced particles to be split roughly 80%/20% in favor of A. However, at any given time, there is uncertainty as to what it is going to do. And, if a simulation were run 10,000 times with this mechanism, the order of production would be likely be different in every instance
    • It is possible to conceive of consciousness as operating according to a probability (e.g. given a certain consciousness in a certain environment, in 80% of cases they may do X, in 15% of cases they may do Y, and in 5% of cases, they may do Z). If consciousness operates according to probability, then randomness/chaos could be emergent, rather than fundamental (i.e. it comes about later, after the initial creation of the universe). Or, randomness could be both fundamental and emergent
      • However, this is not the same as free will. In order to have free will, one would need more than probability. There would need to be some mechanism for the consciousness to at least slightly modify the probabilities of its choices (even if it were just 1%) to have something like free will
  • In order to dogmatically state that determinism is true, one would have to be absolutely certain that there are no probabilistic mechanisms anywhere in the universe, at any time
  • Every effect must have some cause (even if that cause is itself). Events cannot happen without any explanation
    • This principle is not violated by the introduction of "randomness" (i.e. non-determinism). "Random" is different from "uncaused". As discussed above, probabilistic mechanisms can be fully understood, and yet introduce randomness into a system through probability
